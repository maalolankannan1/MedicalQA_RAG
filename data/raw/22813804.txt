1. J Pediatr Surg. 2012 Jul;47(7):1404-9. doi: 10.1016/j.jpedsurg.2012.02.015.

Does obesity impact the pattern and outcome of trauma in children?

Alselaim N(1), Malaekah H, Saade M, Hussein M, Altokhais T, Albedah K, 
Zamakhshary M.

Author information:
(1)King Abdulaziz Bin Saud University for Health Science, Riyadh, Saudi Arabia.

PURPOSE/BACKGROUND: Childhood obesity is pandemic condition. The effect of 
obesity on trauma outcomes in children has been relatively understudied. We 
conducted this study to ascertain the effects of obesity on the hospital outcome 
of injured children.
METHODS: A retrospective cohort study of patients aged 2 to 18 years admitted to 
the King Abdul Aziz Medical City between May 2001 and May 2009 was conducted. 
Patients were categorized as lean (body mass index <95th percentile) and obese 
(body mass index ≥ 95th percentile). Groups were compared regarding admission 
demographics, mechanism of injury, pattern of injury, length of stay, intensive 
care unit admission, ventilation duration, types of procedures performed, injury 
severity score, and mortality.
RESULT: Nine hundred thirty-three patients were included, of those 55 (5.89%) 
children were obese. The obese children were older than nonobese (P = .001) and 
had a higher injury severity score (P = .001) and a lower pediatric trauma score 
(P = .00), heart rate (P = .0081), and respiratory rate (P = .000). There were 
no differences between groups with regard to sex, mechanism of injury, and 
surgical procedures. Obese children were more likely to have rib fractures (P = 
.02) and pelvic injuries (P = .033). There was no significant association 
between mortality and obesity (P = .42).
CONCLUSION: Obesity does not seem to impact the severity of injury, mortality 
rate, types of injury, and procedure outcomes in children. Obese patients are 
more likely to have rib and pelvic injuries.
